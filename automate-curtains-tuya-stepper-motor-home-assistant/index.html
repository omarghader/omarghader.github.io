Automate Your Curtains with a Tuya Stepper Motor and Home Assistant

Automate Your Curtains with a Tuya Stepper Motor and Home Assistant

I’ve always been a fan of waking up to natural light, but dragging myself out of bed to open the curtains felt like a cruel joke. Enter: smart curtains. After some trial and error (and a few hilarious mishaps with overly eager motors), I landed on a reliable setup using a Tuya stepper motor and Home Assistant. Here’s how you can do it too.

Why Automate Curtains?

  • Convenience: Open/close curtains on a schedule or with voice commands.
  • Energy efficiency: Let sunlight warm your room in winter or block it in summer.
  • Pure laziness: Because why not?

What You’ll Need

Hardware

  1. Tuya Stepper Motor (like this one) – The brains and brawn of the operation.
  2. Power Supply (12V, 2A) – To keep the motor running smoothly.
  3. Curtain Rod & Hooks – Ensure compatibility with your motor’s mounting.
  4. Jumper Wires – For connecting everything.
  5. ESP8266 or ESP32 – To bridge Tuya and Home Assistant (optional, but recommended for local control).

Software

  1. Home Assistant – The hub for all your smart home shenanigans.
  2. Tuya Smart Life App – For initial device setup.
  3. ESPHome or Tasmota (optional) – For flashing custom firmware if you want local control.
Pro Tip: If you’re using an ESP8266/ESP32, flash it with ESPHome or Tasmota to avoid cloud dependency. Tuya’s cloud can be... moody.

Step 1: Assemble the Hardware

  1. Mount the Stepper Motor: Attach it to your curtain rod according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Mine required some creative zip-tying, but yours might be more civilized.
  2. Connect the Power Supply: Plug the 12V supply into the motor. Double-check polarity—smoke is not a feature.
  3. Test the Motor: Use the Tuya Smart Life app to pair the motor and verify it moves the curtains smoothly. If it jerks like a startled cat, adjust the rod alignment.

Step 2: Integrate with Home Assistant

Option A: Tuya Cloud Integration (Easy but Cloud-Dependent)

  1. Install the Tuya Integration in Home Assistant:
    • Go to Configuration > Integrations.
    • Search for “Tuya” and follow the setup prompts (you’ll need a Tuya developer account—annoying but necessary).
  2. Once added, your motor should appear as a cover entity. Rename it to something like curtain_master_3000.

Option B: Local Control with ESPHome (Recommended)

  1. Flash an ESP8266/ESP32 with ESPHome:
    esphome:
  name: curtain_controller
  platform: ESP8266
  board: nodemcuv2

wifi:
  ssid: "your_wifi"
  password: "your_password"

# Enable logging
logger:

# Enable Home Assistant API
api:
  password: "your_api_password"

# Stepper motor configuration (adjust pins as needed)
stepper:
  - platform: tuya
    id: curtain_motor
    open_duration: 30s
    close_duration: 30s
  2. Upload the firmware and connect the ESP to the Tuya motor’s control pins (refer to your motor’s datasheet).
  3. In Home Assistant, the curtain should auto-discover as a cover entity. No clouds were harmed in this process.

Step 3: Automate the Magic

Now for the fun part—making your curtains smart:

Example Automation (Morning Routine)

automation:
  - alias: "Open Curtains at Sunrise"
    trigger:
      platform: sun
      event: sunrise
    action:
      - service: cover.open_cover
        target:
          entity_id: cover.curtain_master_3000

Voice Control (Alexa/Google Assistant)

Just expose the entity to your voice assistant via Home Assistant Cloud or Nabu Casa.

Troubleshooting

  • Motor Not Responding?
    • Check power connections.
    • Ensure the Tuya device isn’t stuck in “pairing mode” (flashing lights mean trouble).
  • Home Assistant Not Seeing the Device?
    • Reboot the Tuya hub/ESP.
    • Verify your Tuya API credentials.

FAQ

Can I use this with heavy blackout curtains?

Yes, but ensure your motor is rated for the weight. Upgrade to a beefier model if needed.

What if my Tuya device goes offline?

Switch to ESPHome for local control—cloud outages won’t leave you curtain-less.

Can I add a physical switch?

Absolutely! Wire a momentary switch to the ESP’s GPIO and add it to ESPHome.
